---
title: "Why SQM Mattered More Than Raw Bandwidth"
date: 2026-07-08
author: "cold"
---

THE PROBLEM //

The Internet connection was already fast enough. The more noticeable problem
was what happened while it was busy.

Large downloads or uploads could make ordinary browsing, streaming, and other
interactive traffic feel less responsive. A conventional speed test still
showed a fast connection, but that number did not describe the latency created
when the connection was under load.

THE CHANGE //

The ISP gateway remained upstream, but routing and wireless duties moved to a
TP-Link Archer AX23 running OpenWrt.

Smart Queue Management was enabled on the WAN connection using CAKE. The
configured limits were kept slightly below the connection's observed maximum
throughput so Firelink could manage the queue before uncontrolled buffering
occurred elsewhere in the path.

WHY IT HELPED //

Without effective queue management, one large transfer can occupy the available
buffer and delay smaller, time-sensitive packets behind it.

SQM deliberately controls that queue. This allows interactive traffic to remain
responsive even while the connection is carrying a sustained transfer.

THE TRADE-OFF //

SQM can reduce the highest number shown by a bandwidth benchmark because the
router must keep traffic beneath the configured limits.

That trade was worthwhile. Maximum throughput mattered less than keeping the
connection responsive during normal mixed use.

RESULT //

The practical improvement was immediately more valuable than a slightly larger
speed-test result.

Downloads could continue without making the rest of the network feel congested,
and latency under load became much more consistent.

LESSON //

Connection quality is not defined by raw bandwidth alone.

For this network, managing the queue produced a larger day-to-day improvement
than chasing the highest possible throughput number.
